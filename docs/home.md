# Optimization

Welcome to the optimization volume of the Big Book of Computing. This site is organized as an online book on how optimization emerges across scientific computing, machine learning, inference, and modern data analysis.

Optimization is often introduced as a narrow numerical topic: choose an objective, compute a derivative, iterate until convergence. That view is too small. In practice, optimization is the language used to describe equilibrium, estimation, control, learning, design, and decision-making. This book treats it as a unifying framework that connects geometry, probability, algorithms, and applications.

## What This Book Covers

The material follows four connected arcs:

- Data geometry and statistical structure, which determine what optimization problems actually look like.
- Core optimization methods, from convex formulations and gradient descent to heuristic and combinatorial search.
- Learning and inference methods, where optimization becomes the engine behind estimation, classification, and representation.
- Modern frontier topics, including physics-informed models, graph methods, and transformer-style architectures that depend on large-scale optimization.

## How To Use This Site

This volume supports several reading styles.

- Use the codebook pages to move from theory into implementation and experiments.

If you are new to the subject, start with the [Introduction](chapters/introduction.md), then use the [Contents](chapters/contents.md) page to see the full path through the volume.

## Who This Is For

This book is written for readers who want optimization as a serious computational subject rather than a catalog of isolated algorithms.

- Students in applied mathematics, computational science, data science, and engineering.
- Researchers who need a structured connection between optimization, inference, and scientific modeling.
- Developers and practitioners who use optimization in machine learning, simulation, or control and want clearer conceptual grounding.
- Self-learners who prefer a cumulative book structure over disconnected notes.

## Reading Path

One practical way to move through the material is:

1. Start with Chapters 1 to 3 to understand data geometry, probability, and structure.
2. Continue with Chapters 4 to 8 for the main optimization viewpoints and algorithm families.
3. Use Chapters 9 to 15 to connect optimization with inference, regression, and deep learning.
4. Finish with Chapters 16 to 19 for current research-facing directions and large-scale models.

The current online volume contains 19 chapters, each paired with workbook and codebook material. The progression is cumulative: later chapters assume the conceptual and computational language built earlier in the book.
